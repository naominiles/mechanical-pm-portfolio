Ongoing experiment

Using A.I. in large-scale mechanical PM. Here's what's actually working.

This isn't a feature list or a pitch for any particular tool. It's a running account of where I've found AI genuinely useful in the work — and where it still isn't.

Where I'm using it

Specific workflows, not a general enthusiasm.

Large mechanical projects generate enormous amounts of documentation — submittals, RFIs, meeting minutes, schedules, specs. Most of it is useful. Very little of it gets read carefully. That's the gap where AI tools have started to earn their place in my workflow.

01

Submittal review and cross-referencing

Comparing submittal data against spec sections to catch discrepancies before they reach the GC. What used to take an afternoon now takes about twenty minutes with a reliable LLM and a good prompt.

02

Meeting notes to action items

Turning rough meeting transcripts into structured summaries with owners and deadlines. Mechanical coordination meetings move fast — getting clean documentation out the same day matters.

03

RFI drafting

First drafts of RFIs, especially for repetitive conditions. The framing still needs human judgment, but the time to a clean draft is dramatically shorter.

04

Schedule risk identification

Feeding schedule data and daily logs into a model to surface potential sequence conflicts before they show up in the field. Still early, but promising.

I'm not trying to automate judgment — that's not what this is. The value is in reducing the volume of low-grade cognitive work so there's more bandwidth for the decisions that actually require someone who's been on the job site.

Where it still falls short

Honest about the limits.

Field conditions don't translate cleanly into prompts. When something is going wrong on a job site, the information is partial, the timeline is compressed, and the people involved have context that isn't written down anywhere. AI is not useful there yet. It also hallucinates on technical specs with enough confidence to be dangerous if you're not checking the output. I always check the output.

This page will keep getting updated as the tools improve and my use of them does too.
